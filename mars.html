What Is Mars?

Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun — a cold, rocky, desert world roughly half Earth's size. Its distinctive red colour comes from iron oxide (rust) coating its surface. It is the most Earth-like planet in the solar system: it has a 24-hour day, polar ice caps, seasons, ancient river valleys, and even weather — including the largest dust storms in the solar system.

Yet Mars is also deeply hostile. Its atmosphere is 96% carbon dioxide and only 1% as thick as Earth's — too thin to breathe, too thin to block radiation, and unable to retain enough heat to keep liquid water stable on the surface today. Three billion years ago, however, Mars was a warmer, wetter world — with rivers, lakes, and possibly even a shallow ocean covering its northern hemisphere.

Mars is the most explored planet beyond Earth. Dozens of spacecraft have orbited it, landed on it, and driven across it. It is the top target for human exploration — NASA, SpaceX, and China all have long-term goals of sending astronauts to the Martian surface within the coming decades.

The Martian Landscape — Record-Breaking Geology

🌋

Olympus Mons

21.9km Height
600km Base diameter

The largest volcano in the solar system. At 21.9 km high, it is nearly 3× the height of Mount Everest. Its base is so wide it would cover the entire state of Arizona — or most of France. Its summit caldera is 80 km across. Mars's low gravity and lack of tectonic plates allowed it to grow for billions of years without moving off its hotspot. 🏔️ If you stood at its base, you couldn't see the summit — it's beyond the horizon.

🏜️

Valles Marineris

4,000km Length
7km Depth

The largest canyon system in the solar system. At 4,000 km long, it would stretch from New York to Los Angeles — and back again. Up to 7 km deep and 200 km wide. Earth's Grand Canyon would fit inside a single side valley. It was not carved by a river but formed by tectonic stretching and collapse of the Martian crust billions of years ago.

❄️

Polar Ice Caps

3km Thick (north)
CO₂+H₂O Composition

Both poles have permanent ice caps made of water ice and frozen CO₂ (dry ice). The north polar cap is mainly water ice; the south is mostly CO₂. The caps grow and shrink with the Martian seasons. If the north polar cap melted, it would flood Mars to a depth of about 11 metres across the entire surface.

🌊

Ancient River Valleys

3.5B Years ago
Liquid Water flowed

Orbital imagery shows thousands of ancient dry river channels, lake beds, and delta fans across Mars — clear evidence that liquid water once flowed freely on the surface. Mars Curiosity and Perseverance rovers have driven through ancient lake beds, finding layered sedimentary rock that could only have formed underwater. Mars was once a warm, wet world.

Key Concepts Explained Simply

🎒 Middle School Friendly
🔴

Why Mars Is Red

Mars is covered in iron oxide — rust. Billions of years ago, when Mars had liquid water and a thicker atmosphere, its iron-rich rocks oxidised (reacted with oxygen). The rust was ground into fine dust by impacts and wind. The entire planet is coated in a layer of rusty powder, which gives it its distinctive red colour from space.

Analogies

  • 🔩Rusty nails: Leave iron nails in water and they turn orange-red. Mars did the same thing — just on a planetary scale, over billions of years. The whole planet "rusted."
  • 🏜️Red sand desert: Some deserts on Earth (like parts of Australia) have red sand from iron-rich rocks. Mars is like that desert — except global, frozen, and dead quiet.
  • 🎨Powder pigment: The red isn't solid rock visible from space — it's a fine dust of iron oxide, like red powder paint, coating every surface and constantly being kicked up into the thin atmosphere by wind.
💨

Thin Atmosphere

Mars's atmosphere is only 1% as thick as Earth's — roughly equivalent to what Earth's atmosphere is like at 35 km altitude (the edge of the stratosphere). It's almost entirely CO₂. This means no breathable air, no protection from radiation, and too little pressure to keep liquid water stable on the surface — it would instantly boil or freeze.

Analogies

  • 🏔️Mount Everest summit: At the top of Everest, the air is so thin that humans can barely survive even with oxygen tanks. Mars's surface pressure is roughly 100× thinner than even that.
  • 🫧Almost a vacuum: Earth's atmosphere is like a thick down duvet. Mars's is like a single sheet of tissue paper. It offers almost no insulation, no radiation protection, and no breathable air.
  • 🌡️No thermal blanket: Without a thick atmosphere to trap heat, Mars loses almost all of the Sun's warmth at night. Summer daytime might reach +20°C — but the same night plunges to –80°C.
🌪️

Planet-Wide Dust Storms

Mars experiences the largest dust storms in the solar system — planet-encircling storms that can last weeks or months and completely obscure the surface from orbit. They are triggered when warming dust rises into the atmosphere, causing winds that lift more dust, creating a self-reinforcing cycle. NASA's Opportunity rover was killed by one in 2018 — its solar panels starved of light.

Analogies

  • 🌫️Whole-Earth sandstorm: Imagine a dust storm so large it blanketed every continent, ocean, and mountain on Earth simultaneously — for months. That's what a Martian global storm looks like from space.
  • 🔆Dimmer switch: When a global storm hits, sunlight reaching the surface drops dramatically — like someone slowly turning a dimmer switch down. Solar-powered rovers lose power and can die if it lasts too long.
  • 🧹Self-sweeping floor: The storm lifts its own dust, which cools the surface, creating wind, which lifts more dust — a self-perpetuating cycle. It only stops when the season changes and the Sun angle shifts enough to break it.

Mars Exploration — Key Missions

1976

Viking 1 & 2

NASA · USA

First successful Mars landers. Sent back the first colour photos from the Martian surface. Conducted biology experiments searching for life — results remain debated to this day.

1997

Mars Pathfinder / Sojourner

NASA · USA

First Mars rover — the size of a microwave oven. Proved the airbag landing system worked and demonstrated that rovers could conduct science on Mars. Sojourner drove 100 metres total.

2004

Spirit & Opportunity

NASA · USA

Twin rovers designed for 90-day missions. Opportunity lasted 15 years and drove 45 km. Both found compelling evidence that Mars once had liquid water on its surface.

2012

Curiosity Rover

NASA · USA

Still operating. The size of a car, landed using a sky crane. Has confirmed Gale Crater was once a lake. Detected organic molecules and seasonal methane — neither yet explained. Has driven over 32 km.

2021

Perseverance + Ingenuity

NASA · USA

Perseverance is collecting rock samples for eventual return to Earth. Ingenuity — a small helicopter — became the first powered aircraft to fly on another planet, completing over 70 flights.

2021

Tianwen-1 / Zhurong

CNSA · China

China's first Mars mission — orbiter, lander, and rover in a single launch. Zhurong rover operated for over a year, becoming only the second country to successfully operate a Mars rover.

Could Life Exist on Mars?

Mars is the most promising place beyond Earth in the solar system to search for life — not intelligent life, but microbial life, either ancient or possibly still present. Three billion years ago, Mars had all the ingredients: liquid water, a thicker atmosphere, volcanic heat, and organic chemistry.

Today, liquid water cannot persist on the surface — but it may exist as briny liquid water underground. In 2018, radar instruments on the Mars Express spacecraft detected a signal consistent with a subterranean lake beneath the south polar ice cap — though this remains debated.

Curiosity has detected seasonal pulses of methane in the Martian atmosphere — a gas that on Earth is produced mainly by living organisms. Its source on Mars is unknown. Perseverance is caching rock samples in tubes to be returned to Earth by the 2030s — where scientists can test them for signs of ancient life far more thoroughly than any rover can manage in the field.

🎒 Middle School Analogy

Finding life on Mars would be like finding footprints in a room you thought was always empty. Even if the creature that made them is long gone, the footprints prove it was there. Perseverance is collecting those "footprints" — rock samples that might preserve chemical signs of ancient Martian microbes.

Mind-Blowing Mars Facts

🏔️

Olympus Mons Would Be Invisible Standing On It

Olympus Mons is so wide (600 km across) that if you stood at its base, you couldn't see the peak — it would be beyond the horizon, hidden by the curvature of Mars itself. And if you stood at the summit, the slopes are so gradual that you'd have no sense of being on a mountain at all.

🌅

Sunsets on Mars Are Blue

On Earth, sunsets are red and orange because dust scatters blue light away. On Mars, the effect is reversed — fine iron-oxide dust scatters red light away, making the sky pinkish-red during the day, while sunsets glow a striking blue-grey colour around the Sun — the opposite of what you'd see on Earth.

🪐

Phobos Is Being Torn Apart

Mars's larger moon Phobos orbits so close to Mars — closer than any other moon in the solar system orbits its planet — that tidal forces are slowly shredding it. In about 50 million years Phobos will break apart and Mars will briefly have a ring system, like Saturn's, made from the debris of its own moon.

🚁

We Have Flown a Helicopter on Mars

NASA's Ingenuity helicopter, designed to fly just 5 test flights, has completed over 70 flights on Mars as of 2024. Flying on Mars is extraordinarily difficult — the atmosphere is so thin that rotors must spin at ~2,400 RPM to generate lift (10× faster than an Earth helicopter). It is the first powered flight on another world.
