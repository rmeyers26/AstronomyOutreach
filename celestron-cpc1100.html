Overview

The Celestron CPC 1100 is a flagship consumer Schmidt-Cassegrain telescope, combining an 11-inch (280 mm) aperture with an integrated GPS-enabled computerised mount. It is built for serious amateur astronomers who demand high resolution, portability, and automated alignment.

Its dual-fork alt-azimuth mount and NexStar+ hand controller give observers access to a database of over 40,000 celestial objects — from planetary detail to faint deep-sky galaxies — with one-touch GoTo slewing.

Core Technologies

🛰️

GPS Auto-Alignment

Built-in GPS acquires your exact location and time at power-on, enabling fully automated sky alignment without manual star-hopping. SkyAlign can align using any three bright objects.

🌀

StarBright XLT Coatings

Celestron's proprietary multi-layer optical coatings increase light transmission to over 97.4% per surface, delivering exceptional contrast and true colour fidelity across all wavelengths.

📡

NexStar+ Hand Controller

The backlit 4-inch LCD hand controller features guided tours, real-time sky data, and compatibility with Celestron's WiFi module for smartphone control via the SkySafari or Celestron apps.

🔭

Schmidt-Cassegrain Optics

The SCT design folds a long focal length into a compact tube using a corrector plate, primary mirror, and convex secondary — ideal for planetary observing and astrophotography alike.

📸

Astrophotography Ready

The 9.25-inch rear thread accepts standard camera adapters and T-rings. Add a wedge for polar alignment and the CPC 1100 becomes a capable long-exposure imaging platform.

⚡

AllStar Polar Alignment

Celestron's AllStar routine provides a streamlined polar alignment procedure directly from the hand controller, removing the need for a polar scope or separate alignment tools.

Who It's For

The CPC 1100 is designed for the dedicated amateur astronomer who has outgrown smaller instruments and wants observatory-class performance in a portable package. Its computerised mount removes the barrier of manual star navigation.

It is equally at home at a dark-sky site or in an urban backyard, and transitions gracefully into astrophotography when paired with a focal reducer and equatorial wedge. Expect a learning curve — but a profoundly rewarding one.

Understanding the Optics

🪣

Aperture

280 mm · 11 inches

What does it do?

Aperture is the diameter of the main mirror — basically, how wide the telescope's "eye" opens. The bigger the eye, the more light it catches, and the more detail you can see.

Everyday analogies

🪣 Rain bucket: A wide bucket collects more rainwater than a narrow cup in the same storm. More aperture = more light collected from the same patch of sky.
👁️ Your pupils: In a dark room, your pupils open wide to let in more light. The CPC 1100's 11" mirror is like having a pupil the size of a dinner plate.
🔦 Flashlights: A wide flashlight beam lights up more area than a narrow one. More aperture = more sky "lit up" for your eye to see.
🪐 Saturn comparison A cheap 4" telescope shows Saturn as a blurry oval with rings. The CPC 1100's 11" mirror reveals the Cassini Division — a real gap between Saturn's rings — plus dark cloud belts on the planet itself. That's what more aperture buys you.
📏

Focal Length

2,800 mm (110 in)

What does it do?

Focal length is the distance light travels inside the telescope from the mirror to where the image forms. The longer the journey, the more the image gets stretched — meaning higher magnification (zoom).

Everyday analogies

🛋️ TV distance: Sitting 2 feet from a TV makes it look huge. Sitting 20 feet away makes it look tiny. Focal length is like that distance — longer = bigger image.
📷 Camera zoom lens: When you slide a zoom lens out, the barrel gets longer — that's increasing the focal length. The photo zooms in. Same idea here.
🎯 Hallway trick: Draw a tiny dot on the far wall of a very long hallway. From down the hall it looks bigger than it does up close. More focal length = more magnification of tiny objects far away.
The magnification formula:
Magnification = Focal Length ÷ Eyepiece focal length
2,800 ÷ 25mm eyepiece = 112×
2,800 ÷ 10mm eyepiece = 280×
Swap eyepieces to change the zoom — no moving parts in the telescope itself.
⚗️

Focal Ratio

f/10

What does it do?

Focal ratio = focal length ÷ aperture. For the CPC 1100: 2,800 ÷ 280 = f/10. It tells you how "zoomed in" the telescope naturally is before you even pick an eyepiece. Low number = wide and bright. High number = narrow and zoomed.

Everyday analogies

🧃 Juice funnel: A wide, short funnel pours juice fast (f/4). A narrow, tall funnel pours slowly and in a thin stream (f/10). "Fast" scopes gather light quickly; "slow" scopes zoom in tighter.
🚿 Shower head: Wide spray = covers a big area, low pressure (fast ratio). Narrow jet = covers a small area, high pressure (slow ratio). Same water, different focus.
📸 Phone camera: Portrait mode (blurry background, tight zoom) is like f/10. Landscape mode (wide, everything sharp) is like f/4. The CPC 1100 is built for "portrait mode" of planets.
f/4 — Fast · Wide f/10 ← CPC 1100 f/15 — Slow · Narrow
