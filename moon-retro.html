Surface Terrain

Two Worlds on One Moon

🏔️

Highlands

Terrae · "The Lands"

The ancient, heavily cratered uplands that cover 83% of the lunar surface. Made of light-coloured anorthosite rock, they solidified 4.1–4.4 billion years ago — nearly as old as the solar system itself. Their smooth, rounded mountains have no sharp peaks: without wind, rain, or ice, only meteorite impacts shape them over billions of years.

Dominate the far side — the hemisphere that never faces Earth
Light colour from low-density anorthosite silicate composition
4.1–4.4 billion years old — among the oldest lunar rocks
More heavily cratered than any other terrain — every impact preserved
🌑

Maria

Mare · "The Seas"

The dark splotches visible from Earth — mistaken for oceans by early astronomers. They are vast flat plains of volcanic basalt, created when ancient lava poured into huge impact basins 3.1–3.8 billion years ago. Far fewer craters than the highlands, because the lava covered and erased older impact scars.

Mostly on the near side — the hemisphere that always faces Earth
Dark basalt is chemically similar to Earth's ocean floors
3.1–3.8 billion years old — younger than the highlands
Cover only 17% of the surface, but dominate our view of the Moon

Human Exploration

The Apollo Missions · 1968–1972

APOLLO 8
December 1968

Lunar orbit — no landing

First humans to fly around the Moon. The "Earthrise" photograph — Earth rising over the lunar horizon — became one of the most influential photographs in history.

APOLLO 11
July 20, 1969

Mare Tranquillitatis

First human landing. Neil Armstrong took "one small step for man." 22 kg of samples returned. Buzz Aldrin's footprints remain perfectly preserved in the regolith to this day.

APOLLO 12
November 1969

Oceanus Procellarum

First long-term science station (ALSEP) deployed. Visited the Surveyor 3 lander that had arrived 2.5 years earlier. Showed precise landing was possible.

APOLLO 13
April 1970

Aborted — oxygen tank explosion

An explosion crippled the spacecraft 56 hours into the mission. The crew survived 4 days using the lunar module as a lifeboat — the greatest rescue in space history.

APOLLO 15
July 1971

Mare Imbrium / Hadley Rille

First use of the Lunar Roving Vehicle — the "moon buggy." Astronauts explored 24 km of terrain, visiting Hadley Rille, an ancient channel carved by flowing lava.

APOLLO 17
December 1972

Taurus-Littrow Highlands

Final human Moon mission. First (and only) trained scientist — geologist Jack Schmitt — walked the surface. Returned 111 kg of samples, the most of any mission.

🎒 Middle School Mission Briefing

Key Concepts, Simply Explained

💨

No Atmosphere

The Moon's gravity is only 1/6 of Earth's — too weak to hold onto gas molecules, which escape into space the same way a rocket escapes Earth. No air means no sound, no weather, no sky colour, and wildly extreme temperatures.

Analogies

  • 🎈Balloon grip: Loosen your grip on a balloon and it floats away. The Moon has "loose fingers" — its gravity can't grip gas molecules tightly enough to keep them.
  • 🌡️No blanket: Atmosphere acts like a blanket, keeping Earth's temperature stable. Without one, the Moon bakes at +127°C in sunlight and freezes to –173°C at night.
  • 🔇Soundproof vacuum: Without air, sound has no medium to travel through. On the Moon, astronauts could stand a metre apart and hear nothing without radios.
🔒

Tidal Locking

The Moon takes exactly 27.3 days to rotate AND 27.3 days to orbit Earth. This is called tidal locking — Earth's gravity gradually slowed the Moon's spin over billions of years until it was perfectly synchronised with its orbit. We always see the same face.

Analogies

  • 🚶Walking in a circle: Walk in a circle around a friend while always facing them — you are rotating, but they only ever see your face. That's the Moon's relationship with Earth.
  • 🧲Magnetic snap: Like a clasp that locks two pieces in perfect alignment, Earth's gravity has locked the Moon's rotation — and held it there for billions of years.
  • 🌀Spinning top: Flick a top — it wobbles, then gradually settles into a stable spin. The early Moon "wobbled," then Earth's gravity settled it into a locked, stable rotation.
💥

Impact Craters

A crater forms when a rock from space hits the surface at enormous speed, excavating material in an explosion and leaving a circular depression with a raised rim and sometimes a central peak. On the Moon, craters last billions of years — nothing erases them.

Analogies

  • 🍪Cookie dough: Drop a marble into soft dough and it leaves a round crater with a rim pushed up around the edge — exactly how an impact crater forms, just at a billion-times-larger scale.
  • 📚A history book: More craters = older surface, because craters accumulate over time. The highlands are densely cratered; the maria are not — they were "repaged" by lava 3 billion years ago.
  • 🏫Chalkboard: A wiped board (maria) shows few old marks. A board never erased (highlands) holds every mark ever made over 4 billion years.
🧊

Geologically Dead

The Moon has exhausted its internal heat. No earthquakes, no volcanoes, no tectonic plates moving. Its surface has been essentially frozen in time for 3 billion years — preserving a record of everything that happened to it, including Apollo footprints.

Analogies

  • 🍕Cold pizza: A fresh pizza is hot and bubbling — like an active planet. Left overnight it goes cold and stiff — like the Moon. It still exists, but nothing is happening inside.
  • 🕰️Stopped clock: A geologically dead world is like a clock that ran out of energy — it shows the exact moment it stopped, forever. The Moon's surface is that frozen moment.
  • 🧊Frozen lake: A summer lake has waves and currents. Frozen solid in winter, nothing moves. The Moon's interior is that frozen lake — all geological motion stopped long ago.
💧

Volatile-Poor

The Moon is dramatically short on volatiles — things that evaporate easily at low temperatures, like water, carbon dioxide, and nitrogen. The giant impact that formed the Moon generated such intense heat that these materials boiled away into space and were lost forever.

Analogies

  • 🍳Frying an egg: Crack an egg in a very hot pan — the water evaporates instantly. The Moon's birth was like an infinitely hotter pan: all water and gas were cooked off and never returned.
  • 🌵Extreme desert: A desert is volatile-poor — no water, dry air. The Moon is the most extreme desert imaginable, stripped of every molecule of water during its formation.
  • 🔥Campfire smoke: Water in wet wood escapes as steam. In the Moon's fiery birth, the "smoke" (volatiles) escaped into space — and the Moon had no gravity strong enough to call it back.
🪨

Regolith

The lunar surface is covered in regolith — powdery, shattered rock created by billions of years of meteorite impacts grinding the solid bedrock into fine dust. It's sometimes called "lunar soil" though it has no organic material. Typically several metres deep.

Analogies

  • 🪨Rock in a blender: Imagine grinding rock in a blender for 4 billion years. The result — endlessly broken down by billions of impacts — is regolith: fine grey powder covering the entire Moon.
  • 👣Cement footprint: Press your hand into wet cement and let it set. Apollo footprints in regolith are like that — but the cement set 4 billion years ago, and nothing has disturbed it since.
  • ❄️Undisturbed snowfield: A fresh snowfield keeps every print perfectly. Regolith is the same — no wind, no rain, no erosion means every mark is permanent for millions of years.

Formation Theory

The Giant Impact Hypothesis

Fission Theory

The Moon split off from Earth spontaneously. Rejected — modern physics shows this is impossible, and doesn't explain the Moon's different chemistry.

Rejected
Sister Theory

Moon formed alongside Earth from the same disc of material. Incomplete — cannot explain why the Moon is depleted in iron and volatile compounds.

Incomplete
Capture Theory

Moon formed elsewhere and was captured by Earth's gravity. Rejected — no known mechanism can capture an object this large into a stable orbit.

Rejected
Giant Impact Hypothesis

A Mars-sized body struck early Earth, blasting mantle material into orbit that condensed into the Moon. Explains low iron, volatile depletion, and matching oxygen isotopes.

Best Model

About 4.5 billion years ago, when the inner solar system was still chaotic and crowded, a Mars-sized body — sometimes called "Theia" — struck the young Earth at an oblique angle. The collision released almost enough energy to shatter Earth entirely.

Material from Earth's rocky mantle and the impactor's mantle was blasted into orbit. Computer simulations show that several percent of Earth's mass could have been ejected this way — enough to form a ring of debris around Earth. That ring gradually condensed and accreted into the Moon.

This hypothesis explains three puzzling facts at once: why the Moon has almost no iron core (iron stayed with Earth), why it is depleted in volatiles (the impact's heat boiled them off), and why it has the same oxygen isotope ratio as Earth (its raw material came from Earth's mantle).

🎒 Middle School Analogy

Imagine smashing two balls of clay together at high speed. Some clay flies off and sticks together into a smaller blob orbiting the large one. That smaller blob is the Moon. The "clay" was molten rock, the "smash" was a Mars-sized world, and the result — after 4.5 billion years of cooling — is what you see rising in the sky tonight.

Surface Conditions

Life on the Lunar Surface

🌫️

Regolith — The Lunar Soil

The entire surface is blanketed in powdery regolith — rock ground into fine dust by 4 billion years of meteorite impacts. Typically several metres deep in the maria, up to 10 m in the highlands. Unlike soil on Earth, it contains no organic material — no life has ever enriched it.

👣

Footprints Last Millions of Years

Apollo astronaut footprints will survive millions of years in the regolith, undisturbed. On Earth, footprints are erased by wind and rain within hours. On the Moon there is no weather of any kind — only an occasional meteorite could disturb them.

🌡️

Temperature Extremes

Without atmosphere, surface temperatures swing wildly: +127°C in full sunlight (hotter than boiling water) to –173°C in the lunar night. Each lunar day/night cycle lasts about 29.5 Earth days. Spacesuits must handle both extremes.

💎

Ice at the Poles

NASA confirmed water ice in permanently shadowed polar craters, where temperatures never rise above –250°C. Ice delivered by ancient comets has been preserved there for billions of years. This ice could supply water, oxygen, and rocket fuel for future lunar bases.

Mission Significance

Why the Moon Matters

🕰️

Time Capsule of History

The Moon preserves events erased on Earth by plate tectonics and erosion. Every major period of solar system bombardment is written in the lunar surface — a 4.4-billion-year geological record Earth no longer has.

🌊

Stabilises Earth's Tilt

The Moon's gravity stabilises Earth's axial tilt at ~23.5°. Without it, Earth's tilt could wobble chaotically from 0° to 85° over millions of years — causing climate swings so extreme that complex life might never have developed.

🌊

Drives Earth's Tides

The Moon's gravity pulls Earth's oceans into bulges we call tides. These tides are also slowly pushing the Moon farther away (~3.8 cm per year) while braking Earth's rotation. Coastal tidal rhythms shaped early life on Earth.

🚀

Gateway to Deep Space

NASA's Artemis programme is returning astronauts to the Moon — including the first woman and first person of colour to walk on the surface. Polar ice could supply water, oxygen, and hydrogen fuel for missions to Mars and beyond.

🔭

Perfect Observatory

The far side of the Moon is permanently shielded from Earth's radio interference. Radio telescopes placed there could detect signals from the very earliest moments of the universe — impossible to receive from Earth's surface.

🧪

Blueprint for Planet Formation

The Giant Impact hypothesis reshaped our understanding of how all planets form. The Moon is the best-studied example of planetary formation by collision anywhere in the known universe — our window into how solar systems are built.

Lunar Exploration — Past, Present & Future

Historic Missions
1959

Luna 2 — First to the Moon

Soviet Union

The first spacecraft to reach the Moon, crash-landing into its surface. Proved it was possible to send a spacecraft to another world — a pivotal moment in the Space Race that changed everything that followed.

1966

Luna 9 — First Soft Landing

Soviet Union

First spacecraft to survive landing on the Moon and transmit photos from the surface. Proved the lunar surface was solid enough to support a lander — ending fears that spacecraft would sink into deep dust.

1969–1972

Apollo 11–17 (6 Landings)

NASA

Twelve astronauts walked on the Moon across six missions. Returned 382 kg of lunar samples still being studied today. Apollo 11 (1969) was first; Apollo 17 (1972) remains the last crewed lunar landing to date.

1994

Clementine

US DoD / NASA

First spacecraft to map the entire lunar surface. Provided early radar evidence of water ice in permanently shadowed craters near the poles — a discovery that changed the Moon's strategic value for future exploration.

2008

Chandrayaan-1

ISRO · India

India's first lunar mission. Its Moon Impact Probe was the first Indian spacecraft to land on the Moon. Instruments on board confirmed the presence of water molecules in the lunar soil — a landmark discovery.

2019

Chang'e 4 — Far-Side Landing

CNSA · China

First spacecraft to land on the far side of the Moon — a region never seen from Earth and previously unreachable without a relay satellite. The Yutu-2 rover is still operational, the longest-lived lunar rover in history.

Active Now
2009–

Lunar Reconnaissance Orbiter

NASA

In continuous operation for over 15 years — the longest-running lunar mission. Has produced the most detailed map of the lunar surface ever made, imaging boulders the size of a car, and located the Apollo landing sites.

2023

Chandrayaan-3

ISRO · India

Successfully landed near the lunar south pole — a world first. Confirmed the presence of sulphur and other elements in south-polar soil. Made India the fourth nation to soft-land on the Moon and the first to reach the south pole.

2024

Chang'e 6 — Far-Side Sample Return

CNSA · China

The first mission ever to return samples from the Moon's far side — 1,935 g of ancient material from the South Pole–Aitken Basin, one of the largest impact craters in the solar system. Scientists expect these rocks to reveal secrets of early lunar and planetary formation.

Upcoming
2026

Artemis II — Crewed Flyby

NASA · CSA

Four astronauts — Reid Wiseman, Victor Glover, Christina Koch, and Canada's Jeremy Hansen — will fly around the Moon on a free-return trajectory. The first crewed lunar mission since Apollo 17 in 1972. Not a landing, but humanity's return to cislunar space.

2026

Chang'e 7

CNSA · China

A comprehensive south-pole mission comprising an orbiter, lander, rover, and a small "hopper" that can fly into permanently shadowed craters to search directly for water ice — an essential resource for future lunar bases.

Late 2020s

Artemis III — Crewed Landing

NASA

The first crewed lunar landing since Apollo 17. Will land near the south pole, where water ice in permanently shadowed craters could support a long-term human presence. Uses SpaceX Starship as the lunar lander.
