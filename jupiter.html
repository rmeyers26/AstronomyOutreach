What Is Jupiter?

Jupiter is a gas giant — a planet so massive it contains more than twice the mass of all the other planets combined. Unlike Earth, it has no solid surface. You couldn't land on it; you'd just sink deeper and deeper into swirling gas, until the pressure crushed anything we've ever built.

Its most famous feature, the Great Red Spot, is a storm that has been raging for at least 400 years. At its largest it was three times the size of Earth. Scientists aren't sure why it keeps going — there's nothing to slow it down.

Jupiter also acts as a giant cosmic shield for the inner solar system. Its enormous gravity captures or deflects comets and asteroids that would otherwise head toward Earth. Without Jupiter, our planet would get hit far more often.

What Is Jupiter Made Of?

Hydrogen (gas) 75%
Helium 24%
Methane, ammonia ~0.9%
Other trace gases ~0.1%

Jupiter is almost entirely hydrogen and helium — the same stuff stars are made of. It's often called a "failed star" because if it had been about 80× more massive, it could have ignited nuclear fusion and become a second sun.

Key Concepts Explained

🌀

Gas Giant

Jupiter has no solid ground. It's mostly hydrogen and helium gas that gets denser and hotter the deeper you go, eventually turning into a liquid — and possibly a small rocky core at the very centre.

Analogies

  • ☁️Cloud diving: Imagine jumping into a cloud. At first it's just fog. But imagine it keeps getting thicker and thicker the lower you go — until eventually it's as dense as water, then as dense as rock. That's Jupiter.
  • 🍮Jello cup: The outside is wiggly and soft. Dig deeper and it gets firmer. At the very bottom there might be a solid chunk. Jupiter works the same way — soft outside, unknown solid core inside.
  • 🌊Deep ocean: The ocean has no "floor" you crash into right away — you sink through increasing pressure until you can't go further. Jupiter is like an ocean of gas with no obvious bottom.
🌪️

Great Red Spot

A storm larger than Earth that has been spinning continuously for over 400 years. It's an anticyclone — wind spinning in the opposite direction of most storms — with wind speeds up to 640 km/h.

Analogies

  • 🛁Bathtub drain: Water swirling down a drain keeps spinning in one direction because nothing stops it. On Jupiter there's no land or friction to slow the storm — so it just keeps going and going for centuries.
  • 🍦Soft-serve swirl: The bands of Jupiter swirl past each other like two conveyor belts moving in opposite directions. Where they meet, the gas gets spun up into a permanent vortex — the GRS.
  • ⏰Old timer: The GRS started spinning before the United States existed. It was already a storm when Isaac Newton was writing his laws of physics. That's how long it's been going.
🧲

Gravity & Magnetic Field

Jupiter's gravity is 2.4× Earth's — if you weighed 50 kg on Earth, you'd weigh 118 kg on Jupiter. Its magnetic field is the strongest of any planet — 20,000× stronger than Earth's — trapping deadly radiation around it.

Analogies

  • 🏋️Heavy backpack: If you normally carry a 10 kg backpack and suddenly it became 24 kg, every step would be exhausting. Jupiter's gravity would make walking feel like carrying double your own weight.
  • 🫧Giant bubble: Jupiter's magnetic field creates an invisible bubble (the magnetosphere) so big that if you could see it from Earth, it would appear larger than the full Moon in our sky.
  • 🚗Cosmic vacuum: Jupiter hoovers up stray asteroids and comets with its gravity, like a car driving through bugs on a highway. Earth is safer because Jupiter cleans up the neighbourhood.
⚡

Fastest Spin in the Solar System

Despite being the largest planet, Jupiter spins a full rotation in just 9 hours 56 minutes. This creates powerful forces that flatten the planet at its poles and bulge it at its equator — Jupiter is noticeably squashed.

Analogies

  • 🍕Pizza dough: A pizza maker spins dough in the air and it flattens and spreads outward. Jupiter spinning so fast does the same — it squashes at the top and bottom and puffs out at the middle.
  • 🎡Spinning top: A fast-spinning top wobbles and the edges flare out. Jupiter is like a top the size of 1,300 Earths, spinning so fast its equator bulges 9,000 km more than its poles.
  • 🌀Washing machine: A washing machine on spin cycle pulls everything outward. Jupiter's spin creates centrifugal force that pushes its atmosphere toward the equator, creating those dramatic bands.
⚗️

Liquid Metallic Hydrogen

Deep inside Jupiter, the pressure is so extreme that hydrogen gas is squeezed into a liquid that conducts electricity like a metal. This bizarre substance — found nowhere on Earth — is what generates Jupiter's massive magnetic field.

Analogies

  • 🧃Squished juice box: Squeeze a juice box hard enough and liquid squirts out the sides. Jupiter squeezes hydrogen so hard it transforms into a completely different state — one that behaves like liquid metal.
  • 💎Diamond from coal: Coal and diamonds are both made of carbon — but extreme pressure turns one into the other. Extreme pressure turns Jupiter's hydrogen into something that behaves like a metal.
  • 🔌Electric river: This liquid metallic hydrogen flows in currents like an underground river, and those moving electric currents create a giant magnetic field — like how your phone's motor creates a magnetic field when current flows through it.
🛡️

Jupiter: Earth's Bodyguard

Jupiter's massive gravity attracts comets and asteroids, pulling them into itself or slinging them out of the solar system before they can reach Earth. Scientists estimate Jupiter reduces asteroid impacts on Earth by a factor of 1,000.

Analogies

  • 🎳Bowling pin: Imagine Jupiter is a giant bowling ball rolling around the solar system. Comets and asteroids are like pins — when they get too close, they get knocked away before they can reach the lanes where Earth lives.
  • 🪰Bug zapper: A bug zapper attracts insects with its light and zaps them before they reach you. Jupiter's gravity attracts space rocks and destroys or deflects them before they reach the inner planets.
  • 🏈Offensive lineman: In football, linemen block defenders before they reach the quarterback. Jupiter is Earth's offensive lineman — blocking cosmic threats before they get close.

The Galilean Moons — Discovered in 1610

🌋

Io

Most Volcanic World Known

Io has hundreds of active volcanoes — more than any world in the solar system. Jupiter's gravity constantly squeezes and stretches Io, creating enormous heat inside. It's essentially a ball of lava wrapped in a thin crust.

🍊 Squeeze an orange back and forth in your hands — the inside heats up from friction. Jupiter squeezes Io the same way, but the heat is so intense it erupts as volcanoes.

🧊

Europa

Possible Hidden Ocean

Europa is covered in a smooth shell of ice — but beneath it may lie a liquid water ocean twice the size of all Earth's oceans combined, warmed by tidal heating. It's one of the best candidates in the solar system for finding life.

🏒 Imagine a hockey rink with a warm underground lake beneath the ice. Europa might be like that — a frozen surface hiding a vast ocean below where life could exist.

🌑

Ganymede

Largest Moon in Solar System

Ganymede is bigger than the planet Mercury and has its own magnetic field — the only moon in the solar system known to have one. It likely also has a subsurface ocean beneath its icy crust.

🪐 Ganymede is so big that if it orbited the Sun instead of Jupiter, astronomers would call it a planet. It's a world in its own right.

🌘

Callisto

Most Cratered World Known

Callisto is one of the most heavily cratered objects in the solar system — its surface is ancient and unchanged. Every impact from billions of years is still visible. Scientists use it as a "geological clock" to measure solar system history.

📖 Callisto's surface is like a history book that was never erased — every page (crater) represents a different event in the solar system's 4.6-billion-year story.

Mind-Blowing Jupiter Facts

💎

It Rains Diamonds

Deep in Jupiter's atmosphere, lightning storms turn methane into soot (carbon). As the soot sinks through intense pressure, it crystallises into graphite, then diamond. Scientists estimate it rains about 1,000 tonnes of diamonds per year. They never reach the surface — they melt back into carbon in the extreme heat below.

🌪️

A Storm Older Than America

The Great Red Spot has been observed since at least 1665 — that's before the United States existed, before Mozart was born, before the steam engine was invented. It's a single storm that has been continuously raging for over 350 years. On Earth, the strongest hurricanes last a few weeks at most.

⭐

Almost a Star

Jupiter is made of hydrogen and helium — exactly what the Sun is made of. If Jupiter had been about 80 times more massive, its core would have reached temperatures high enough to ignite nuclear fusion and become a star. Our solar system would have had two suns, and Earth's sky would never go fully dark.

📡

It Broadcasts Radio Waves

Jupiter emits powerful radio waves — so powerful they can be picked up by a backyard radio antenna on Earth. The waves are created by electrons spiralling along Jupiter's massive magnetic field lines. You can literally listen to Jupiter with the right equipment, and it sounds like crashing waves and electronic noise.
